    Chỉ Đặng

    I have been living in the U.S. for less than a year. Before coming to Helping Link, I was still getting adjusted to this new life. Thankfully, my daughter referred over to Helping Link as she herself also took an ESL class there a few years prior. It was at Helping Link where I met a lot of very nice tutors who were not only helpful but also very relatable. Although, my English still has some progress to go, I have been able to retain basic words and initiate a few conversations. While I had studied English in Vietnam, a major accident left me with impaired memory. However, through Helping Link’s classes and patience, I have been able to make more progress than I thought I could have done. For that, I am very grateful for the opportunity Helping Link has given me and allowing me to spend my time to learn a new language in this new life.

    Si Dinh

    I started my first day at Helping Link back in July 2017. English has been my biggest hurdle thus far as I have just immigrated to the U.S. from Vietnam a few months before with my family. After hearing about Helping Link from a few family members, I decided it was time for me to learn English and hopefully be able to communicate to my extended family better, especially those who have grown up here. The best thing I like about the ESL classes here about Helping Link is that the lessons are interactive and everyone in class is very friendly and welcoming. I would like to continue attending class to gain better knowledge and improve my English fluency.

    Lieu Nguyen

    I first came to Helping Link about two years ago to learn English just like everyone else. I found that grasping the language myself was too much for me to communicate fluently. During the quarter, I really liked my teacher, Leilani, as she helped me a lot. I found that lessons involving topics such as transportation and banking very valuable and applicable to my life here in U.S. I learned a lot of practical skills and words that helped me become more independent and resourceful. Taking ESL at Helping Link has motivated me to learn more.

    In King County, 20% of of residents are foreign-born. Of those residents, 43% speak English less than ‘very well.’ English language skills are crucial for refugees and immigrants: they are what allow children to succeed in school, and adults to find employment.

    Our ESL program provides at least one native English speaker and one bilingual English and Vietnamese speaker with clients, placing an emphasis on conversation practice and social interaction. Topics vary as we tailor lessons to match the needs of our clients. Volunteer tutors serve as facilitators to help each client reach their particular goals.

    Clients work with an instructor in Helping Link’s desktop computer lab to learn basic skills like how to turn the machine on/off, access the internet, search for directions on a mapping site, create an email address, log in/out of email, and send/receive mail. These skills increase clients’ employability, support their independence, and allow them to communicate more easily with family at home.

    Helping Link is more than a place where clients come to take classes. It’s a safe space for clients to gather, share resources, and access information in their native language.

    We provide a directory of resources to clients seeking guidance in basic social service needs. These resources include but are not limited to Employment, Immigration, Education, Housing, Childcare, Medical/Dental Health, Legal Services and Transportation.

    iPad classes aim to empower students with skills to help with employability, self-sufficiency & workplace advancement.

    Our iPad basics and photo/video classes are very hands on! Most ESL adults find a keyboard interface to be challenging since they are not familiar with the language or the keyboard layout. A touch navigation system introduces basic technology concepts necessary to navigate the internet and use email or social media to stay connected with family.
